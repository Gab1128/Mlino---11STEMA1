Logarithmic Laws and Properties

-Logarithmic laws and properties are mathematical rules that govern the manipulation and simplification of logarithmic expressions.
Logarithms are mathematical functions that represent the exponent to which a fixed number (called the base) must be raised to obtain a given number.
The logarithmic laws and properties make it easier to perform calculations involving logarithms. Here are some fundamental logarithmic laws:

Properties of Logarithms:

Definition:

If by=x, then logbx=y. In other words, the logarithm of x to the base b is equal to y.

Zero Property:

logb1=0. The logarithm of 1 to any base is always 0.

Identity Property:

logbb=1. The logarithm of a number to the same base is always 1.

Inverse Property:

The inverse of logbbx=x is blogbx = x. Likewise for natural logarithms, the inverse of lnex=x is elnx=x.

Change of Base Formula

When the original logarithm cannot be solved as is. We use the change of base formula. The change of base formula is denoted by logba = logda/logdb. The new base is found by determining a similar base between the argument and the original base.

Undefined Logarithms:

logbx is undefined for x≤0. Logarithms of non-positive numbers are undefined.

Laws of Logarithms

1. Product Law:

logb(xy)=logb(x)+logb( y ) This law states that the logarithm of the product of two numbers is equal to the sum of the logarithms of the individual numbers.

2. Quotient Law:

logb(x/y)=logb(x)−logb(y) The logarithm of the quotient of two numbers is equal to the difference of the logarithms of the individual numbers.

3. Power Law:

logb(xn)=n⋅logb (x) This law states that the logarithm of a number raised to a power is equal to the product of the exponent and the logarithm of the base.
